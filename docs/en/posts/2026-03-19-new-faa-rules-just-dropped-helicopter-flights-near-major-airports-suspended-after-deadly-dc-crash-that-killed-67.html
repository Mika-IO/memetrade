NEW FAA Rules JUST Dropped! Helicopter Flights Near Major Airports SUSPENDED After Deadly DC Crash That Killed 67

The Skies Above America's Airports Just Changed

The Federal Aviation Administration is implementing sweeping new safety rules in congested airspace around major airports, including the suspension of visual separation between planes and helicopters. The changes are a direct response to last year's catastrophic collision between an American Airlines jet and a U.S. Army Black Hawk helicopter near Washington, D.C., which killed 67 people.

What's Changing

The new regulations represent the most significant overhaul of airport airspace rules in years:

  • Visual separation suspended — controllers can no longer rely on visual separation alone to keep aircraft apart in high-traffic corridors.
  • Helicopter restrictions — helicopter operations near major airports face new altitude and routing limitations.
  • Enhanced radar requirements — additional technological safeguards are being mandated for approach and departure corridors.

The Political Dimension

The rule changes come as the FAA faces intense political scrutiny from both parties. The agency has been under pressure to act decisively since the January 2025 disaster, and these regulations represent the most concrete safety improvements to date.

Aviation safety experts have broadly welcomed the changes, though some argue they should have been implemented immediately after the crash rather than months later.
